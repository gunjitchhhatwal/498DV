How Content drove the Netflix growth story!

Netflix started as a DVD rental business but its founders never wanted it to be their company's destination. Its founders saw internet as a medium to decentralize entertainment and unbundle premium television from the grip of big cable companies.

In 1997, Netflix launched a video library of approximately 900 titles.By April 1999, it's video library expanded to 3100 titles and by January 2000 it had 5200 titles.

In 2007 Netflix launches it's online streaming platform "Watch Now". This laid the foundation stone of the behemoth that Netflix was to become in the coming years and this is where out story begins.

Once internet became their primary delivery medium, Netflix understood the importance of continously adding content to its library so that people don't run out of things to watch and this became the primary reason for their unprecedented subscriber base growth.

Netflix had invested $40M in the development of new streaming technologies in 2007, the year when Netflix launched its streaming service "Watch Now", even though it's core business of DVD rentals was growing.

Because hardly anybody thought it would work, even fewer companies actively pursued it. By the time everybody else caught on, Netflix had the best streaming technology and the largest library of titles.

The company was also able to negotiate cheap licensing deals with networks like Starz and in 2008 it gave Netflix access to 2500 titles from the network.

By 2012, Netflix's relationships with several studios and media publishers had become strained and the deal it had with Starz was canceled. Netflix lost access to 2500 titles that it was able to add to it's list from this deal.

This set the stage for the most important transformation that Netflix was about to go through, from a content delivery platform to one of the largest content creator in the world.

2013 saw Netflix dive headfirst into the world of original programming with its high-profile political drama, House of Cards. Netflix had finally begun to realize its ambitions of being a one-stop-shop for original content, distributed via it's proprietary platform

Although transitioning from licensing content to producing its own movies and TV shows might seem counterintuitive for an engineering company, it made perfect sense for Netflix. The more content Netflix produced, the more subscribers it attracted. This, in turn, resulted in higher revenues, which meant more funding for original content in a virtuous cycle of constant growth.

In 2016, Netflix went live in 130 counties simultaneously and in 2017 it's subscriber count surpassed that of all the cable networks combined in the US.

All this while Netflix was investing the most in acquiring/creating content by as much as double the amount its competitors were investing. It's aggressive approach in adding content to it's platform was only paralleled by the it's subscriber base growth.
